\begin{abstract}
    The effect sizes of trait-associated variants exhibit across many traits a common pattern of decreasing magnitude as a function of minor allele frequency.
    This is assumed to be the result of negative selection where natural selection acts stronger against large effect variants, dragging their frequency downwards.
    To account for this phenomenon, it is common to model the variance of effect sizes to be inversely proportional to heterozygosity, namely the $\alpha$-model.
    Although the $\alpha$-model captures the inverse relationship of effect size and minor allele frequency to some extent,
    the modeling choice is arbitrary and lacks a population genetic foundation.
    To this end, we derive an alternative to the $\alpha$-model to address the frequency-dependent pattern of effect sizes from an evolutionary framework, whereby a focal trait is subject to selection indirectly due to a correlated trait experiencing stabilizing selection.
    The new model consists of three interpretable parameters, namely the mutational variance, selection intensity, and mutational correlation.
    These parameters split into two identifiable components that can be estimated conveniently through the restricted maximum likelihood estimator.
    Furthermore, we demonstrate the practical utility of the model by showing that it improves genetic prediction by 10 to 20\% compared to $\alpha$-model in forward simulations. 
\end{abstract}

\vspace{1em} 
\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} stabilizing selection; polygenic trait, statistical genetics

\section{Introduction}

Stabilizing selection has recently emerged as a powerful theoretical framework to understand trait-associated loci in human genetics.
By considering a special instance of Fisher's geometric model (FGM) with isotropically distributed mutational effects,
[Simons et al 2018, cite] made predictions about the distribution of genome-wide association study (GWAS)-derived effect sizes.
This meant that the contribution of complex traits to fitness can be estimated from GWAS data, which was done in a follow-up study by the same authors [cite].
In another work utilizing FGM, [Koch et al 2024, cite] argued that stabilizing selection is a primary mode of selection acting on human traits and further estimated its strength.
These works show that linking evolutionary theory directly to statistical genetics can unveil the evolutionary landscape of complex trait from data.
The aforementioned studies propose ways to analyze GWAS results, 
but does not explicitly tell how their models can be fit to raw genotype data.

% opening
From a more statistical genetics viewpoint, one notable finding from massive GWAS studies is the negative correlation between effect sizes and allele frequency [cite].
This is usually understood in the context of negative selection where large effect variants are more likely to be removed from the population than small effect variants.
A common modelling choice is the $\alpha$-model, where the variance of the effect size is inversely proportional to heterozygosity raised to the power $0\le \alpha \le 1$.
This model first appeared in the setting of estimating genetic relatedness matrices from single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) markers and has later been extended to explicitly detect negative selection [cite].
Nevertheless, it is largely based on statistical curve-fitting and no population genetic model has yet been discovered to back this model.

% apparent stabilizing selection
In this work, 
we propose a general procedure to combine an evolutionary model to mixed-effects models in statistical genetics,
and apply it to populations under stabilizing selection.
This yields a linear mixed model parameterized by evolutionary quantities that can be estimated through a conventional statistical method, namely the restricted maximum likelihood (REML) estimator.
These quantities appear in the variance of random effects encoding the aforementioned frequency dependence of effect sizes that is distinct from the $\alpha$-model.
Unlike the $\alpha$-model, the new formula does not diverge to infinity as the sample frequency approaches zero and allows unbiased estimation of mutational variance.
Furthermore, as a mixed-effects model, it can estimate genetic values from best unbiased prediction (BLUP).

\section{Theory and model}

\subsection{Diffusion approximation for stabilizing selection}

Fisher's geometric model (FGM) assumes that 
an individual's fitness is given by
\begin{align} \label{eq:fitness}
    \exp \left(
        -\frac{\norm{z}^2}{2V_S} 
    \right)
\end{align}
where $z=(z_1, \ldots, z_d)$ is an $d$-dimensional trait vector subject to stabilizing selection and $V_S$ controls selection intensity.
The marginal selection coefficient $\seff$ acting on a given locus is
\begin{align} \label{eq:seff}
    \seff(x) = \left(x-\frac{1}{2}\right)\frac{\norm{\alpha}^2}{V_S}
\end{align}
for a biallelic loci of frequency $x$ and effect size $\alpha=(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_d)$ with each entry corresponding to $z_i$. \citep{Robertson1956againstextreme,Lande1976phenoevolution,Simons2018popgeninterpretation, Negm2024longrangeld}.

In a panmictic diploid population of size $N$,
the diffusion approximation corresponding to equation~\ref{eq:seff} is
\begin{align} \label{eq:wf_diffusion_raw}
    \begin{aligned}
    dX_t &= (\seff(X_t)X_t(1-X_t) + \mu(1-2X_t))dt + \sqrt{\frac{X_t(1-X_t)}{2N}}dB_t \\
    &= \left[ X_t(1-X_t)\left(X_t-\frac{1}{2}\right)\frac{\norm{\alpha}^2}{V_S}
    + \mu(1-2X_t) 
    \right] 
    dt + \sqrt{\frac{X_t(1-X_t)}{2N}}dB_t
    \end{aligned}
\end{align}
where $B_t$ is the Brownian motion.
We choose to measure $V_S$ in units of $2N$,
so we write $V_S = 2N \cdot W_S$.
Accordingly, we keep $\theta = 4N\mu$.
By scaling equation~\ref{eq:wf_diffusion_raw} by the $2N$ factor,
we obtain
\begin{align} \label{eq:wf_diffusion_scaled}
    dX_t = \left[
    X_t(1-X_t)\left(X_t-\frac{1}{2}\right)\frac{\norm{\alpha}^2}{W_S} 
    + \frac{1}{2}\theta(1-2X_t)
    \right] 
    dt + \sqrt{X_t(1-X_t)}dB_t   
\end{align}
with the forward Kolmogorov equation given by
\begin{align} \label{eq:fwd_kolmogorov_scaled}
    \begin{aligned}
     \pard{p(x,t;\alpha)}{t} 
     =& -\pard{}{x} 
     \left[
     \left(
        x(1-x)\left(x-\frac{1}{2}\right)
        \frac{\norm{\alpha}^2}{W_S}
        + \frac{1}{2}\theta(1-2X_t)
        \right)
        p(x,t;\alpha)
     \right] \\
     &+\frac{1}{2} \pard[2]{}{x}[x(1-x)p(x,t;\alpha)].
     \end{aligned}
\end{align}

We'll make use of the stationary density $p(x,t;\alpha)$ of equation~\ref{eq:fwd_kolmogorov_scaled}.
This can be obtained by setting the left-hand side to zero, meaning that the density does not change with respect to $t$ \citep{Ewens2004mathpopgen}.
\begin{align} \label{eq:stat_dist}
     p_{\infty}(x;\alpha) = C(\alpha)^{-1} \cdot
     x^{\theta-1}(1-x)^{\theta-1} 
     \exp\left[-\frac{\norm{\alpha}^2}{W_S}x(1-x) \right]
\end{align}
with the normalizing constant 
\begin{align} \label{eq:norm_const}
    C(\alpha) = \int_0^1 x^{\theta-1}(1-x)^{\theta-1} 
    \exp\left[-\frac{\norm{\alpha}^2}{W_s}x(1-x) \right] dx = B(\theta, \theta) 
    \cdot {_1F_1} \left(
    \theta; \theta + \frac{1}{2}; -\frac{\norm{\alpha}^2}{4W_S}
    \right)
\end{align}
where $_1F_1(a;b;z)$ is the Kummer's hypergeometric function.
Fortunately, $_1F_1(a;b;z) = 1 + \mathcal{O}(a)$, so we can ignore the factor as $\theta \rightarrow 0$ [cite].
Hence,
\begin{align}
    p_{\infty}(x ; \alpha) \ \propto \
    x^{\theta-1}(1-x)^{\theta-1}
    \exp\left[-\frac{\norm{\alpha}^2}{W_s}x(1-x) \right]
\end{align}
after suppressing $C(\alpha) \approx B(\theta, \theta)$.

\subsection{Connecting evolution to mixed-effects models}

Genomic mixed-effects models mainly consider the conditional distribution of $y$ given $G$.
If $y$ is the focal trait and $\beta$ is the corresponding effect size, we assume that
\begin{align}
    y = G\beta + \veps.
\end{align}
Accordingly, the variance decomposition of the trait is
\begin{align}
    \begin{aligned}
    \var{y \mid G} &= \var{G\beta + \veps \mid G} \\
    &= G\var{\beta \mid G}G^T + \var{\veps \mid G} \\
    &= G\var{\beta \mid G}G^T + \var{\veps} 
    \quad
    (\because G \indep \veps) \\
    &= G \Sigma_\beta G^T + \var{\veps}
    \end{aligned}
\end{align}
where $\Sigma_\beta = \var{\beta \mid G}$.
A common model choice is the $\alpha$-model
\begin{align}
    \begin{aligned}
        \Sigma_\beta = \textrm{diag}
        \left( 
        \frac{h^2}{M} \cdot \frac{1}{[p_j(1-p_j)]^\alpha}
        \right) 
    \end{aligned} 
\end{align}
where $M$ is the number of variants and $h^2$ is a positive constant often referred to as the single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) heritability.
Here, the effect of $G$ enters the formula by it's column-wise frequency $p_j$.

In practice, we only have the sample frequency $\hp_j$ instead of $p_j$, so the estimate
\begin{align} \label{eq:alpha}
    \begin{aligned}
        \widehat{\Sigma}_{\beta} = \textrm{diag}
        \left( 
        \frac{h^2}{M } \cdot \frac{1}{[\hp_j(1-\hp_j)]^\alpha}
        \right) 
    \end{aligned}
\end{align}
replaces $\Sigma_\beta$.
The $\alpha=1$ case is the genome-wide complex trait analysis (GCTA) model [cite, Yang].
Together with $G$, $G \widehat{\Sigma}_{\beta}G^T$ is a genomic estimate of genetic relatedness, called the genomic genetic relatedness matrix (GRM) [cite].
For a more comprehensive review of different choices of $\Sigma_\beta$, see [cite].

Despite its empirical success [cite], 
equation~\ref{eq:alpha} and its variants have several theoretical and practical shortcomings.
In particular, one assumes the formula $\Sigma_\beta = \var{\beta \mid G}$ at the beginning, 
so the parameters $h^2$ and $\alpha$ are merely tuning parameters without an explicit biological interpretation, although heuristic arguments exist.
For instance, a positive $\alpha>0$ partially captures the inverse relationship between minor allele frequency and effect size [cite].
A notable pathological behavior is that $[p_j(1-p_j)]^{-\alpha}$ diverges to infinity as $p_j$ approaches zero, requiring heuristic GRM partitioning over multiple MAF bins if rare variants are involved [cite, Wainschtein].
Also, it is unclear if the sample frequency $\hp_j$ can replace the true frequency $p_j$, especially in rare variants that tend to exhibit larger sampling variance.

Our framework offers a principled solution to the aforementioned issues.
The key is to \emph{deduce}, instead of assuming, a formula for $\Sigma_{\beta,j} = \var{\beta_j \mid G}$ from evolutionary theory where $j$ denotes the $j$-th locus.
A common assumption in the literature is that $\beta_j$'s mean is zero,
thereby we get $\var{\beta_j \mid G} = \E{\beta_j^2 \mid G}$.
Then, we can derive $\E{\beta_j^2 \mid G}$ from
\begin{align} \label{eq:goal2}
    \E{\beta_j^2 \mid G} = \E{\E{\beta_j^2 \mid G, \norm{\alpha_j}} \mid G} = \E{\E{\beta_j^2 \mid \norm{\alpha_j}} \mid G} = \int 
    \E{\beta_j^2 \mid \norm{\alpha_j}} \cdot p(\alpha_j \mid G) d\alpha_j
\end{align}
where $\alpha_j$ is the effect size \emph{vector} of the $j$-th locus (previously we used the subscript $i$ on $\alpha$ to denote the corresponding $i$-th trait).
What is left is to determine $\E{\beta^2 \mid \alpha}$, which measures much the focal trait's effect $\beta$ is determined by the overall fitness effect $\alpha$,
and $p(\alpha  \mid G)$, the likely value of overall fitness given the genotype data
from a given evolutionary model.
The following section will explain how this is done in the case of stabilizing selection.
Once these steps are done, we can use restricted maximum likelihood (REML) estimator to estimate the parameters appearing the model.

\subsection{Fitness effect and its influence on a focal trait}

We'll assume in the rest of the paper the following:
\begin{align} \label{eq:alpha_to_beta}
    \E{\beta^2 \mid \norm{\alpha}} = \sigma_b^2 (1- \rho_{ab}^2) + \rho_{ab}^2 \cdot \frac{\sigma_b^2}{\sigma_a^2} \cdot \norm{\alpha}^2
\end{align}
for some constants $\sigma_a^2$, $\sigma_b^2$ and $\rho^2$.
Although the choice looks arbitrary at first sight, 
the relationship of [Simons et al, 2018]'s framework is a special instance of this parameterization.
Under their isotopic FGM model,
\begin{align}
    \E{\alpha_1^2 \mid \norm{\alpha}} = \cdots =
    \E{\alpha_d^2 \mid \norm{\alpha}}
\end{align}
holds by symmetry (all $\alpha_i$ has the same expected magnitude) and 
\begin{align}
    \sum_{i=1}^d \E{\alpha_i^2 \mid \norm{\alpha}} = \norm{\alpha}^2
\end{align}
gives $\E{\beta^2 \mid \alpha} = \norm{\alpha}^2 /d $ where $\beta$ is one of the $\alpha_i$.

Another scenario we may consider that yields equation~\ref{eq:alpha_to_beta} is when the fitness effect is drawn from a multivariate normal distribution
\begin{gather} \label{eq:coa}
\begin{pmatrix}
        \alpha \\
        \beta
    \end{pmatrix}
    \sim
    N\left(
    \begin{bmatrix}
        0 \\
        0
    \end{bmatrix}
    , 
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_{a}^2 & \rho_{ab}\sigma_{a}\sigma_{b} \\
        \rho_{ab} \sigma_{a} \sigma_{b} & \sigma_{b}^2
    \end{pmatrix} 
    \right)
\end{gather}
and selection is acting on a one-dimensional trait with fitness effect $\alpha$.
Then, $\sigma_a^2 = \E{\norm{\alpha}^2}$, $\sigma_b^2=\E{\beta^2}$, and $\rho_{ab}^2 = \mathrm{corr}(\norm{\alpha}^2, \beta^2)$ from the property of multivariate normal distributions.


\subsection{Deriving the approximate genetic architecture}

As we fixed $\E{\beta_j^2 \mid \norm{\alpha_j}}$ to be a linear function of $\norm{\alpha_j}^2$ from equation~\ref{eq:alpha_to_beta},
completing equation~\ref{eq:goal2} reduces to finding
\begin{align}
    \E{\norm{\alpha_j}^2 \mid G}.
\end{align}
As in previous works, we assume linkage equilibrium and simplify the above to
\begin{align}
    \E{\norm{\alpha_j}^2 \mid G_j}
\end{align}
to relax the dependence of the effect size at locus $j$ only to the corresponding $j$-th column of $G$.
Then,
\begin{align} \label{eq:bayes_for_alpha}
    \begin{aligned}
        p(\norm{\alpha_j}^2 \mid G_j) \ \propto \
        p(G_j \mid \norm{\alpha_j}^2) \cdot p(\norm{\alpha_j}^2).
    \end{aligned}
\end{align}

The first term of the product is
\begin{align}
    \begin{aligned}
    p(G_j \mid \norm{\alpha_j}^2)    
    &=
    \int_0^1 
    p(G_j \mid x_j)
    p_{\infty}(x_j \mid \alpha_j)
    dx_j \\
    &=
    \int_0^1 
    \binom{2n}{k_j} 
    x_j^{k_j}(1-x_j)^{2n-k_j}
    \pinf(x_j \mid \alpha_j) 
    dx_j \\
    & \ \propto \ \int_0^1   
    \binom{2n}{k_j} 
    x_j^{k_j}(1-x_j)^{2n-k_j}
    x_j^{\theta-1}(1-x_j)^{\theta-1}
    \exp\left[-\frac{\alpha_j^2}{W_S}x_j(1-x_j) \right]dx_j \\
    &\ \propto \ \exp\left[-\frac{\alpha_j^2}{W_S}\hp_j(1-\hp_j) \right]
    \end{aligned}
\end{align}
where $k_j$ is the number of derived alleles and $n$ is the number of samples.
This expression is identical to the site frequency spectrum (SFS).

Equation~\ref{eq:bayes_for_alpha}'s second moment is easy to enumerate analytically if we assume that $\norm{\alpha_j}^2$ follows a $\chi^2$-distribution multiplied by $\sigma_a^2$.
Then $\E{\norm{\alpha_j}^2} = \sigma_a^2$ and
\begin{align}
    p(\norm{\alpha_j}^2 \mid G_j) \ \propto \ \exp\left[-\frac{\norm{\alpha_j}^2}{W_S}\hp_j(1-\hp_j) \right] \cdot
    \frac{1}{\norm{\alpha_j}}\exp\left(-\frac{\norm{\alpha_j}^2}{2\sigma_a^2}\right).
\end{align}
The desired quantity is then
\begin{align} \label{eq:alpha2_cond_avg}
    \E{\norm{\alpha_j}^2 \mid G_j} =
    \frac{\sigma_a^2}{
    1 + 2\frac{\sigma_a^2}{W_S}\hp_j(1-\hp_j)
    }.
\end{align}
Lastly, we substitute equation~\ref{eq:alpha2_cond_avg} to equation~\ref{eq:alpha_to_beta}
\begin{align} \label{eq:beta_cond_var}
    \E{\beta_j^2 \mid G_j} = 
    \sigma_b^2 \cdot
    \left(
        1 - \rho_{ab}^2 \cdot 
        \frac{
        2 \frac{\sigma_a^2}{W_S}\hp_j(1-\hp_j) 
        }{
        1+ 2 \frac{\sigma_a^2}{W_S}\hp_j(1-\hp_j)
        }
    \right).
\end{align}


\section{Simulation and empirical study}

\subsection{SLiM forward simulation}

We evaluate all of our theory in forward simulations conducted by SLiM [Haller and Messer 2025, cite].
This is in contrast to common practices benchmarking linear mixed models that use fixed genotypes with varying only the random effects and the residual noise of the regression model [cite].
We do not simulate data directly from formula~\ref{eq:arch_final}.
Instead, we conduct an individual-based simulation in SLiM that is under stabilizing selection according to the fitness function in equation~\ref{eq:fitness}.
The simulation is based on equation~\ref{eq:coa} where a one-dimensional trait is subject to selection while the observed trait is correlated to it by its focal effect sizes.

The precise simulation configuration is as follows.
The population followed a panmictic demographic history with a constant diploid population size of $N=10{,}000$ individuals. 
Each genome was of length $L=2 \times 10^6$ with mutation rate of $\mu=10^{-8}$ and recombination rate of $r=10^{-5}$.
The configuration was borrowed from [Hayward and Sella, 2022] to set $L\mu=0.02$ mutation per genome per generation, $2NL\mu = 400 \gg 1$ to ensure polygenicity, and $r/\mu = 10^3$ for low linkage disequilibrium.
The mutational effect sizes $\alpha$ were simulated from $\sigma_a^2 \in \{0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$ and the selection width $W_S= V_S/2N =\{1.0, 2.0, 4.0\}$.
As $V_S$ is of scale $\mathcal{O}(N)$, the environmental variance $V_E$ and the genetic variance $V_G$ were negligible.
Hence, the fitness function~\ref{eq:fitness} was used in the simulations.
For each pair of $\sigma_a^2$ and $W_S$, 
a single burn-in of $10N$ generations were taken followed by 20 separate replicates of $5N$ generations.

To simulate apparent selection, for each population after total $15N$ generations, we drew the observed effect sizes $\beta$ conditional on $\alpha$ according to the joint distribution in equation~\ref{eq:coa}.
This is same as sampling $\beta$ during SLiM simulation because $\beta$ has no effect on the simulation at all ($\alpha$ does the job).
For $\sigma_b^2$ and $\rho_{ab}^2$, we chose it to be the same as $\sigma_a^2$ and in a grid of $\{0.00, 0.50, 1.00\}$, respectively.

\subsection{Variance component estimation}

We first examined whether REML recovers the variance components implied by equation~\ref{eq:beta_cond_var}. Because the frequency-dependent part of the model is identified through the product $\rho_{ab}^2 \sigma_a^2 / W_S$, the most informative target is the ratio $\sigma_a^2 / W_S$ whenever $\rho_{ab}^2 > 0$. Figure~\ref{fig:blm_sigma2_a_over_ws} shows that the fitted values preserve the ordering of the true parameter combinations across the simulation grid, but they lie systematically below the naive reference $\sigma_a^2 / W_S$. The discrepancy is most visible when selection-induced frequency dependence is strongest. At the same time, the estimates are substantially closer to the Bulmer-corrected reference lines than to the uncorrected target, indicating that the main departure from the single-locus theory is consistent with attenuation from linkage disequilibrium rather than a failure of the functional form itself. As expected, the $\rho_{ab}^2=0$ panels are uninformative, because selection on the latent trait does not induce frequency dependence in the focal trait when the two traits are uncorrelated.

The overall scale parameter $\sigma_b^2$ is considerably easier to recover. Figure~\ref{fig:blm_sigma2_b} shows that REML estimates of $\sigma_b^2$ remain centered near the true value across the full grid of $W_S$ and $\rho_{ab}^2$, with only modest replicate-to-replicate variability. Taken together, these results suggest that the focal-trait variance is well identified, whereas the selection-sensitive component is recovered chiefly through the combined quantity $\rho_{ab}^2 \sigma_a^2 / W_S$ and is therefore more sensitive to departures from the single-locus approximation.

\subsection{Genetic prediction}

We next compared held-out BLUP accuracy under the evolutionary model and three $\alpha$-model baselines. Figure~\ref{fig:blup_prediction} reports the validation $R^2$ across the same grid of simulation parameters. The strongest pattern is the poor performance of the $\alpha=1$ model, which underperforms substantially in every regime. The $\alpha=0.5$ model is closer, but still remains below the evolutionary fit throughout most of the grid. By contrast, the evolutionary model and the $\alpha=0$ model are often quite similar, especially when $\rho_{ab}^2=0$, where equation~\ref{eq:beta_cond_var} reduces to an approximately frequency-independent architecture. When the coupling between the focal and selected traits is stronger, the evolutionary model typically retains a small but repeatable advantage.

The distinction is sharper when one examines the variance components obtained from the prediction fits. In Figure~\ref{fig:sigma2_b_estimates}, the evolutionary model remains close to the true $\sigma_b^2$ throughout the simulation grid. The $\alpha=0$ model is approximately unbiased only when $\rho_{ab}^2=0$ and becomes downward biased once the focal trait inherits frequency dependence from the selected trait. The $\alpha=0.5$ and $\alpha=1$ fits underestimate $\sigma_b^2$ much more severely in all scenarios. Hence, the similarity in predictive accuracy between the evolutionary model and $\alpha=0$ in some panels should not be interpreted as recovery of the same architecture: the heuristic model can predict reasonably well while still mischaracterizing the underlying variance components.

Appendix Figures~\ref{fig:blup_prediction_rho1_fixed} and~\ref{fig:sigma2_b_estimates_rho1_fixed} examine a practical simplification in which the evolutionary fit fixes $\rho_{ab}^2=1$. Held-out prediction is nearly unchanged relative to Figure~\ref{fig:blup_prediction}, and estimation of $\sigma_b^2$ remains close to the full model, although with somewhat greater dispersion in some settings. This supports the reduced parameterization as a useful approximation when separate identification of $\rho_{ab}^2$ and $\sigma_a^2 / W_S$ is weak.

\section{Discussion}
% opening

We presented a general procedure to derive evolution-aware mixed-effects model.
We applied the framework to stabilizing selection with pleiotropy and 
found a novel frequency-dependence formula of effect sizes expressed in terms of evolutionary parameters.
The parameters can be estimated by restricted maximum likelihood (REML) informing the underlying fitness landscape of the trait.
Practically, common applications of mixed-effects models such as genetic prediction by best linear unbiased predictor (BLUP) can be performed.
When applied to forward simulations,
our model performed better than the conventional $\alpha$-model across $\alpha$-values in prediction tasks, 
further supporting the utility of the model.

\paragraph{The pleiotropy model}
Empirical studies have shown that pleiotropy is pervasive in human complex traits.
[Simons et al 2018, cite] models it using Fisher's geometric model (FGM) with isotropic mutations effects.
In this framework, each axis of the multi-dimensional trait contributes equally to the fitness, 
forcing the mutation's effect on a focal trait to spread evenly across axes.
In equation~\ref{eq:alpha_to_beta}, we generalize this relationship to account for potentially non-isotropic relationship between a focal trait and the underlying fitness.
One example is the jointly Gaussian mutational effects that has been considered in earlier theoretical works and in which we considered in our simulations [cite].
It would be interesting to investigate alternative models of pleiotropy that may lead to the relationship of equation~\ref{eq:alpha_to_beta}.

% reml is moment-based so its okay
\paragraph{Identifiability and estimation}
In checking REML's ability to estimate parameters,
we found that jointly estimating $\rho_{ab}$ and $\sigma_a^2/W_S$ is very difficult.
This is because $\hp_j(1-\hp_j)$ are usually too small and not variable enough due to the $U$-shaped distribution of allele frequencies.
To see this, the denominator of equation~\ref{eq:beta_cond_var} is mostly
\begin{align}
    1 + 2\frac{\sigma_a^2}{W_S}\hp_j(1-\hp_j) \approx 1,
\end{align}
so $\rho_{ab}$ and $\sigma_a^2/W_S$ mostly appear as a product in the numerator
\begin{align}
    \rho_{ab}^2 \cdot 
    \frac{
    2 \frac{\sigma_a^2}{W_S}\hp_j(1-\hp_j) 
    }{
    1+ 2 \frac{\sigma_a^2}{W_S}\hp_j(1-\hp_j)
    } \approx
    2 \left(
    \frac{\rho_{ab}^2\sigma_a^2}{W_S}
    \right) \hp_j(1-\hp_j).
\end{align}
A temporary fix is to fit a model by assuming $\rho_{ab}=1$ and treat the resulting estimate as $\rho_{ab}^2\sigma_a^2/W_S$.
In the appendix, we found that this degrades the estimator's precision but has negligible impact on genetic prediction by BLUP.
We also find that estimation of $\sigma_b^2$ remains stable despite this slight misspecification.

\paragraph{Simplifying assumptions}
In order to derive a simplistic formula, 
we made various assumptions.
These include genotype likelihood factorization mirroring composite likelihood, population reaching stationarity, low mutation rate, and weak selection relative to recombination.
In the most general setting, the dynamics of a focal locus may depend on the remaining loci through linkage (we ignore epistasis as we focus on a strictly additive model), which is ignored by the factorization.
This allowed us to reduce our analysis to a single locus.

\paragraph{Frequency-dependence and genetic prediction}
We found that the evolutionary model estimates the focal-trait variance parameter $\sigma_b^2$ correctly and achieves the best prediction in forward simulations.
Interestingly, the $\alpha=0$ model shows near-identical prediction accuracy while degrading in $\sigma_b^2$ estimation.
The first result can be explained by the low frequency of most variants, making $\E{\beta_j^2 \mid \hp_j} \approx \E{\beta_j^2}$.
This holds more most variants, the genetic relatedness matrix (GRM) of the methods are almost identical up to a constant factor.
As BLUP is invariant with respect to constant scaling of GRM, the two are likely to coincide.
The reduction in $\sigma_b^2$ is also expected because population's genetic variance is reduced by selection.
Since $\alpha=0$ has no frequency-dependence encoded into the model, the model compensates the reduced genetic variance by lowering its $\sigma_b^2$ estimate.

The two nonzero $\alpha$-models shows notable degradation in prediction accuracy and $\sigma_b^2$ inference.
The result clearly shows that evolutionarily principled modeling of frequency-dependence has an advantage over statistical heuristics.





\paragraph{Linkage disequlibrium and the Bulmer effect}
The main text entirely ignores linkage disequilibrium (LD) and subsequently the Bulmer effect.
We attribute the consistent under-estimation of $\sigma_a^2/W_S$ across parameter configurations to the Bulmer effect.
In short, Bulmer effect creates dragging forces opposed to selection, weakening the effective strength of selection acting on the locus.
This was formalized by a [Negm and Veller 2026, cite] who showed that the reduction of selective forces can be expressed as a multiplicative factor.
Hence, algebraically, this has the same effect as an increased value of selection width $W_S$, thereby smaller $\sigma_a^2 /W_S$.
By expanding their formula with further approximations, we found that Bulmer effect precisely predicts the degree of under estimation.
See appendix for more details.

\section{Mean-field approach to linkage disequilibrium}

So far, we completely ignored the effect of linkage which creates theoretical challenges.
To analyze the dynamics of an individual locus in general,
we have to consider the countless number of interactions with other loci that exhibit randomness.
To simplify the problem, we adopt the \emph{mean-field} approximation where the locus is assumed to interact with the rest of the system in a simplified fashion.
Here, the field is the rest of the genome, or the genetic background, with which the focal locus interacts.
Because this background consists of a huge number of loci,
although being stochastic in principle, we instead treat it as a deterministic \emph{average} which gives rise to the term mean-field.

By working with each locus while leaving all else as a deterministic aggregate, 
it gives rise to the factorized probability in equation~\ref{eq:mean_field}.
Under this framework, one can further refine equation~\ref{eq:seff} as (after scaling by $2N$)
\begin{align} \label{eq:seff_ld}
    \seff(x_j) = \left(x_j-\frac{1}{2}\right)\cdot \left( 
    1- \frac{1}{2\overline{c}_j}\frac{V_g}{V_S}
    \right)^2 \cdot \frac{\alpha_j^2}{W_S} 
\end{align}
where $\overline{c}_j$ and $\overline{c}$ are harmonic means of the recombination fraction given by
\begin{gather}
    \overline{c}_j = (L-1) \bigg/ \sum_{k:k \neq j} \frac{1}{c_{jk}}
    \quad \text{and} \quad
    \overline{c} = L(L-1) \bigg/ \sum_{j,k:i \neq j} \frac{1}{c_{jk}}
\end{gather}
and $V_g = 2\sum_j x_j(1-x_j)\alpha_j^2$ is the genic variance of $z$ as derived in [Negm and Veller 2026, cite].
For simplicity, let $u_j$ be the normalized position of locus $j$ so that $Lu_j$ is the position in base pairs and write $h(u_j) = 1\, / \, 2\overline{c}_j$.

The difficulty arising in equation~\ref{eq:seff_ld} is that $V_g$ is also a random variable as it's a function of $x_j$ that are random.
We take another round of mean-field approximation so that $V_g = 2L \cdot \E{x(1-x)\alpha^2}$ is a constant.
By further assuming that mutation rate is uniform, otherwise we can rescale the genome,
we arrive at
\begin{gather}
    \begin{gathered}
        \alpha \sim N(0, \sigma_a^2) \\
        u \sim \mathrm{Uniform}[1/L,(L-1)/L] \\
        x \mid \alpha, u \sim x^{\theta-1}(1-x)^{\theta-1}
        \exp\left[
            - \left(
            1- h(u) \cdot \frac{V_g}{V_S}
            \right)^2 \cdot 
            \frac{\alpha^2}{W_S}
            x(1-x)
        \right] \\
        V_g = 2L \cdot \E{x(1-x)\alpha^2}.
    \end{gathered}
\end{gather}
under stationarity as before.
Solving for $V_g$ with $h(u) V_g \ll V_S$ gives
\begin{align}
    V_g = \frac{2L\theta \sigma_a^2}{2 + \frac{\sigma_a^2}{W_S}}.
\end{align}
which means that the shape of $h$ is irrelevant under the $V_g \ll V_S$ approximation.
A locus-level equivalent is derived in [Keightley and Hill 1988, cite].
With diminishing selection $W_S \rightarrow \infty$, the formula reduces to the classic neutral result $V_G = L\theta\sigma_a^2 = 4NL\mu\sigma_a^2$ [Lynch 1986, cite].

We now follow the same steps as before to deduce
\begin{align} \label{eq:arch_final}
    \begin{aligned}
        \E{\beta_j^2 \mid \hp_j, u_j}
    &= \sigma_{b,j}^2 \cdot 
    \left[
    1 - 
    \rho_{ab,j}^2 \cdot 
        \frac{
             2\left(1-h(u_j)\frac{V_g}{V_S}\right)^2\frac{\sigma_{a,j}^2}{W_S} \hp_j(1-\hp_j)}{
            1 + 2\left(1-h(u_j)\frac{V_g}{V_S}\right)^2\frac{\sigma_{a,j}^2}{W_S} \hp_j(1-\hp_j) 
        }
    \right]
    \end{aligned}
\end{align}
which reduces to
\begin{align}
    \begin{aligned}
        \E{\beta_j^2 \mid \hp_j, u_j}
    &=   
        \frac{
        \sigma_{b,j}^2
             }{
            1 + 2\left(1-h(u_j)\frac{V_g}{V_S}\right)^2\frac{\sigma_{a,j}^2}{W_S} \hp_j(1-\hp_j) 
        }
    \end{aligned}
\end{align}
if $\rho=1$, a perfect correlation between $\alpha$ and $\beta$.
As before, if $\rho=0$, it simply becomes $\sigma_{b,j}^2$ as selection on $z$ has no influence on $y$ at all.
For the correction factors in Figure~\ref{fig:blm_sigma2_a_over_ws}, we used the global harmonic mean of recombination fraction.
